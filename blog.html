How to Talk to Your Barber

And walk out with the exact cut you pictured. No jargon, no guessing.

Barbering 101By the Goodfellas crew·About a 6 minute read

Here is the truth most guys never hear: the cut you get is only as good as the conversation you have before the cape goes on. At Goodfellas, our barbers will always ask questions and steer you right, but the more you can put your look into words, the closer you land to the picture in your head. So here is the plain-English guide we wish every new client walked in with.

Prefer to listen?

The short version, from two of us at the shop. About two minutes.

Start with the picture, not the number

The single most useful thing you can bring to the chair is a photo. One or two reference shots tell your barber more in three seconds than five minutes of describing ever could. Pull up a picture of a cut you like, or even an old photo of yourself on a good hair day, and we will tell you honestly what will work with your hair type and head shape and what we would tweak.

If you do not have a photo, that is fine too. Just describe the everyday look you are going for. Do you want low maintenance that grows out clean, or something sharp you will keep up every couple of weeks? That one answer changes everything we do next.

Fades, tapers and what the difference actually is

People use these words like they mean the same thing. They do not, and knowing the difference will get you a long way.

  • A taper is subtle. The hair gets gradually shorter around the ears and the neckline, but it never goes down to skin. It is clean and conservative, great if you want a cut that still looks sharp grown out a bit.
  • A fade blends your hair from longer on top down to very short, and a skin fade takes it all the way down to bare skin. It is a bolder, higher-contrast look.
  • Low, mid or high tells us where the fade starts. A low fade hugs the ears and neckline. A mid fade starts around the temples. A high fade starts up near the crown for maximum contrast. When in doubt, a mid fade is the crowd pleaser.

You do not have to memorize any of this. Say "I want it tight on the sides but not bald" or "blend it down to skin," point to where you want it to start, and we take it from there.

Talk about the top in inches or finger-lengths

For the length on top, numbers help. Clipper guards go up in eighths of an inch, so a number two is a quarter inch, a number four is half an inch, and so on. If you are getting scissor work on top, tell us in finger-widths or just say "take off about an inch" or "keep most of the length, just clean it up." And speak up about the parts of your routine that matter: how you part it, whether you blow dry it, how much time you actually spend on it in the morning. A great cut fits your real life, not a magazine.

Beards: lines are everything

A beard trim is not just about length. The lines do the heavy lifting. Tell your barber where you want your cheek line, whether you want it natural or sharp, and how low you want the neckline to sit. We finish beard work with hot lather and a straight razor on the edges, which is what makes a beard look intentional instead of just grown out. Mention if you have a cowlick, a patchy spot or a scar to work around. We have seen it all and we will build the shape around it.

The hot towel shave is worth it

If you have never had a traditional hot towel straight razor shave, put it on your list. Hot towels open everything up, warm lather goes on, and a true straight razor gives you the closest, smoothest shave there is, finished with a cooling aftershave. It is part grooming, part fifteen minutes of doing absolutely nothing. Guys book it for weddings and big days, and plenty come back just because it feels that good.

Bringing the kids? Just say so

Goodfellas is a family room, and we mean it. Plenty of our regulars bring their teen boys and their little ones. If it is a kid's first real haircut, tell us, and we will go slow, keep it relaxed, and send them out looking sharp with a story to tell. There is no such thing as too young for a good first chair.

A few small things that make a big difference

  • Come in with clean-ish hair if you can. It is easier to see your natural fall and cut it accurately.
  • Speak up mid-cut. If you want it shorter, tighter or want to change course, say so. We would much rather adjust as we go than have you walk out unsure.
  • Tell us about the next few weeks. Got a wedding, a trip or a job interview? That helps us decide how short to go and how it should grow out.
  • Ask about product. We carry our own beard and hair products and we are happy to show you how to get the look at home in under a minute.

Walk in or book? Either works

You never need an appointment at Goodfellas. Walk-ins are always welcome, and on a normal day you will get in with little or no wait. That said, if you have a barber you have clicked with, or you are squeezing a cut into a tight schedule, booking a chair online lets you lock in your person and your time so you are in and out without watching the clock. Both get you the same care in the chair, so do whatever fits your day.

One more thing worth knowing: this is a shop that remembers you. Tell us your name, your barber will remember your fade, your part and the way you like your neckline, and the conversation picks up right where it left off. That is the part you cannot get from a chain, and it is exactly why people drive in from across the Treasure Valley to sit in our chairs.

The Goodfellas way

At the end of the day, a barbershop is about trust. Our barbers, some of the best from Boise to Caldwell, take the time to learn how you like your cut so that by your second or third visit you barely have to say a word. Until then, bring a photo, describe your real routine, and do not be shy about the details. That is how you walk out of Goodfellas looking, as one of our regulars put it, like a million bucks.
